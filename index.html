Lesson 4

Line of Best Fit and Making Predictions

General Mathematics 11 | Fourth Quarter

S.Y. 2025-2026

Learning Objectives

At the end of this lesson, you should be able to:

  • Understand the concept of the Line of Best Fit and the Method of Least Squares.
  • Calculate the equation of the least-squares line of best fit using summary statistics.
  • Draw the line of best fit on a scatterplot.
  • Make predictions using the equation of the line.
  • Distinguish between interpolation and extrapolation and understand their reliability.

1. The Line of Best Fit

If the points on a scatterplot tend to lie along a straight path, we can fit a line to the data to model the relationship. The process of fitting this straight line is known as linear regression.

Equation of a Straight Line:

$$ y = mx + c $$
  • $y$ = dependent variable (vertical axis)
  • $x$ = independent variable (horizontal axis)
  • $m$ = slope - how steep the line is.
  • $c$ = y-intercept - where the line crosses the y-axis.

A straight line approximating the data

2. Method of Least Squares

Drawing a line "by eye" isn't reliable because everyone might draw it slightly differently. To find the exact mathematical "best fit", we use the Method of Least Squares.

You can calculate the exact equation of the line, $y = mx + c$, using summary statistics about the data.

The Formulas

1. Calculate the Slope, $m$:

$$ m = r \frac{s_y}{s_x} $$

2. Calculate the y-intercept, $c$:

$$ c = \bar{y} - m\bar{x} $$

Where:

  • $r$ = Pearson's correlation coefficient
  • $s_x$ and $s_y$ = standard deviation of $x$ and $y$
  • $\bar{x}$ and $\bar{y}$ = mean (average) of $x$ and $y$

Guided Example: Calculating the Equation

The heights ($x$) and masses ($y$) of 9 people have been recorded. The following statistics were determined:

$\bar{x} = 176.5556$
$s_x = 8.6681$
$\bar{y} = 67$
$s_y = 6.6833$
$r = 0.9743$

Step 1: Calculate the slope ($m$)

$$ m = r \frac{s_y}{s_x} $$ $$ m = 0.9743 \times \frac{6.6833}{8.6681} $$ $$ m = 0.9743 \times 0.7710 \approx 0.7512 $$

Step 2: Calculate the y-intercept ($c$)

$$ c = \bar{y} - m\bar{x} $$ $$ c = 67 - (0.7512 \times 176.5556) $$ $$ c = 67 - 132.6286 = -65.6286 $$

Step 3: Write the full equation

$$ y = 0.7512x - 65.6286 $$

Weight = $0.7512 \times$ Height $- 65.6286$

Try This! Calculate the Equation

Given the following summary statistics for a dataset, find the equation of the least-squares line of best fit (round to four decimal places):

$\bar{x} = 11.38$
$s_x = 1.87$
$\bar{y} = 230.7$
$s_y = 97.87$
$r = 0.94$

Step 1: Calculate $m$

$$ m = r \frac{s_y}{s_x} = 0.94 \times \frac{97.87}{1.87} $$ $$ m = 0.94 \times 52.3369 \approx 49.1967 $$

Step 2: Calculate $c$

$$ c = \bar{y} - m\bar{x} = 230.7 - (49.1967 \times 11.38) $$ $$ c = 230.7 - 559.8584 = -329.1584 $$

Final Equation:

$$ y = 49.1967x - 329.1584 $$

Guided Example: Drawing the Line

How do we draw $y = 0.7512x - 65.6286$ on a scatterplot? Pick two $x$ values, find their $y$ values, plot the points, and connect them.

1. Choose $x = 170$

$y = 0.7512(170) - 65.6286$

$y = 127.7040 - 65.6286 = \mathbf{62.0754}$

Point 1: (170, 62.0754)

2. Choose $x = 180$

$y = 0.7512(180) - 65.6286$

$y = 135.2160 - 65.6286 = \mathbf{69.5874}$

Point 2: (180, 69.5874)

4. Making Predictions: Interpolation vs. Extrapolation

Once we have the equation of the line, we can substitute an $x$ value to predict a $y$ value. But not all predictions are equally reliable.

Interpolation Extrapolation
Predicting values within the range of the given dataset. Predicting values outside the range of the given dataset.
Highly Reliable (if the data has a strong linear association). Less Reliable / Risky. We don't know if the trend continues outside the data we've observed.

Extrapolation Limitations

Extrapolating too far from the dataset can lead to illogical or impossible predictions. Let's look at an example.

Example: Student Age vs. Height

We measured the heights of students aged 7 to 16. The equation of the line of best fit is found.

  • Predicting height at age 12: This is Interpolation. The prediction will likely be very accurate because 12 is between 7 and 16.
  • Predicting height at age 30: This is Extrapolation. If we plug $x = 30$ into the child's growth equation, it might predict the person is 260 cm (8 feet 6 inches) tall!

Why did it fail? Adult height does not grow at the same rate as a child. Using the model to extrapolate far outside the data is flawed!

Try This! Making Predictions

The equation relating a person's weight (in kg) to shoe size is given below. This equation is only accurate for shoe sizes between 6 and 12.

$$ \text{Weight} = 2.2000 \times \text{Shoe Size} + 48.1000 $$
  1. What is the predicted weight of a person with a shoe size of 7? Is this interpolation or extrapolation?
  2. What is the predicted weight of a person with a shoe size of 5? Is this interpolation or extrapolation?

Question 1 (Shoe size 7):

Weight $= 2.2000(7) + 48.1000 = 15.4000 + 48.1000 = \mathbf{63.5000 \text{ kg}}$

Interpolation (because 7 is between 6 and 12).

Question 2 (Shoe size 5):

Weight $= 2.2000(5) + 48.1000 = 11.0000 + 48.1000 = \mathbf{59.1000 \text{ kg}}$

Extrapolation (because 5 is outside the 6 to 12 range).
